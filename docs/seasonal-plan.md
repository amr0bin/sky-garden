# Seasonal plan

*How I build my sky garden gradually, so there's always something alive and working — and so no single season ever asks too much of me at once.*

The aim is a garden that is never fully asleep. Vancouver's mild coastal winters (roughly zone 8b) make this achievable on a balcony: evergreen structure, winter berries for the birds, early bulbs, and a long flowering season from spring through autumn can all overlap. The plan below moves through a first full year, then suggests how to keep growing in the years after.

My guiding principle for containers on a north-eastern, sunny balcony: **the biggest pots I can carry and afford, in a material that steadies root temperature** — glazed ceramic, timber, or fabric rather than thin plastic. Morning sun and open air dry pots quickly, so watering is the daily discipline; and because potted roots are less insulated than those in the ground, the few genuinely cold nights each winter are when a little protection matters.

---

## Spring — the foundation season

Spring is when most of the work and most of the joy is. Plant the herbs and flowers now, and settle in the young trees and shrubs while they are still dormant or just waking.

**Plant:** the anchor trees and shrubs (crab apple, blackthorn, hawthorn, rowan, elder) — bare-root or potted, ideally before they fully leaf out. Sow or plant out the easy herbs: calendula, yarrow, nettle (in its own contained pot), comfrey, and lemon balm — and tuck wild garlic and primrose into a cooler, shadier pot.

**Active now:** blackthorn opens its white blossom on bare black wood — the earliest of all — and primrose gives the first nectar; nettle's young tops and wild garlic's leaves are ready as the first spring greens; crab apple, hawthorn, and rowan come into blossom for the bees; calendula, comfrey, and lemon balm begin their long season.

**Tend:** top up and refresh the potting mix in established containers, begin regular feeding as growth speeds up, and set out a shallow water dish for the pollinators.

---

## Summer — the season of medicine

This is the harvest season for most of the herbs, and the peak for the pollinators.

**Plant:** heather (for late forage and winter structure), meadowsweet and water-mint (both kept damp — water-mint in a saucer of water), vervain, valerian, chamomile, and St John's Wort. Add foxglove for the bees and the lore — to admire, never to harvest.

**Active now:** elder and meadowsweet flower in early summer; St John's Wort opens at midsummer for its red oil; yarrow, calendula, comfrey, vervain, chamomile, and lemon balm are all working; foxglove's bells and lemon balm draw the bees.

**Tend:** water deeply and consistently — this is the season pots dry out. Harvest herbs on a dry morning after the dew lifts. I begin my first simple preparations (a calendula oil, a yarrow salve) — see the [preparation guide](preparation-guide.md).

---

## Autumn — the season of the harvest and the birds

The fruit and berries ripen, feeding both the still-room and the birds, and autumn is the best time to plant anything woody.

**Plant:** autumn is ideal for establishing trees and shrubs — roots settle over the cool, wet months and are ready to grow in spring. A good moment to add to the collection.

**Active now:** crab apple, hawthorn haws, rowan, and elderberries all ripen — some for me (cooked), some left for the birds; blackthorn's sloes wait for the first frost to sweeten for cordial; valerian's root is ready to lift, clean, and dry; heather flowers latest of all.

**Tend:** make elderberry syrup and rowan or crab-apple jelly. Dry the last of the summer herbs for winter tea. Ease off feeding as growth slows, and move the most tender pots closer to the shelter of the wall.

---

## Winter — the quiet, watchful season

The garden rests, but it is not empty. This is the season of structure, of berries for the birds, and of planning.

**Active now:** heather stays green and low; rowan and crab-apple fruit persist for the birds through the cold; the trees' bare shapes give the balcony its winter form.

**Tend:** on the coldest nights, cluster the pots together against the wall and wrap the least hardy — this shared shelter is the potted garden's substitute for deep ground. Keep water available for the birds when it freezes. I clean and oil my tools, and plan next year's additions: which plant, which story, which corner.

---

## Growing on, year to year

The twenty I've already gathered give me the Druids' three sacred herbs (meadowsweet, vervain, and water-mint), the salve and wound herbs, the berrying trees, the earliest and latest bloom, and a harvest in almost every month. Once they are thriving and the rhythm of watering, harvesting, and preparing feels familiar, add a few plants each year rather than all at once — my garden and my knowledge deepen together. Natural next additions from the same tradition include **mugwort** for its dreaming-and-protection lore, **cowslip** as another fairy flower of the field, **betony** — a great protective all-heal of the old herbals — **elecampane** for the chest, and **angelica** or **sweet cicely** for scent and for the bees. Each new record can be added to [`data/plants.json`](../data/plants.json), with a harvest entry and (optionally) a photo in [`../images/`](../images/), and it will appear in the tour.
