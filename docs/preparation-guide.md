# Preparation guide

*How I turn my garden's harvest into simple home preparations — teas, oils, salves, syrups — and how I keep it safe.*

This is a plain guide to the core methods of home herbalism. None of it is medical advice; it records traditional techniques for interest and for careful personal use. Read the safety section first — it is the part that matters most.

---

## First, the safety that governs everything

- **Identify with certainty.** I never prepare a plant I cannot name with confidence. Some medicinal plants have toxic look-alikes.
- **Foxglove is not medicine here.** It is in the garden for its lore and its bees only. No part of it is ever prepared or taken. The same caution applies to any plant marked toxic.
- **One new thing at a time.** With a plant that's new to me, I use one preparation on its own first, so that if I react, I know the cause.
- **Patch-test every topical.** Before using any oil, salve, or wash on a real need, test a little on the inside of the forearm and wait a day.
- **Respect the actives.** A few plants here are genuinely potent or interacting — **hawthorn** (heart), **St John's Wort** (many drug interactions and sun-sensitivity), **meadowsweet** and **yarrow** (salicylates; caution in pregnancy). For internal use of these, I check with a pharmacist or a qualified practitioner, especially since a medication or pregnancy would change things.
- **Comfrey is topical only.** Use it on unbroken skin; do not take it internally.
- **Cook what needs cooking.** Elderberries and rowan berries must be cooked; raw elder (and its leaves, bark, stems) is toxic.
- **Harvest kindly and cleanly.** Pick on a dry morning after the dew has lifted, take no more than a third of any plant, avoid anything sprayed or beside a busy road, and never strip a plant bare.
- **Label everything** with the plant, the preparation, and the date.

## The core preparations

### Infusion (tea) — for leaves and flowers
Pour just-boiled water over the dried or fresh herb (a rough guide: a teaspoon of dried, or two of fresh, per cup), cover, and steep 10–15 minutes. Covering matters — it keeps the volatile oils in the cup. Good for yarrow, meadowsweet, heather, hawthorn flower-and-leaf, nettle, chamomile, lemon balm, water-mint, and (bitter, so usually blended) vervain.

### Decoction — for tougher material
Berries, bark, and hard roots need simmering rather than steeping. Cover with water, bring to a gentle simmer for 15–20 minutes, then strain. This is the method for hawthorn berries and for cooking down elder and rowan.

### Infused oil — the base of every salve
Fill a clean, dry jar loosely with the dried herb, cover completely with a good oil (olive or sweet almond), and either stand it on a warm, sunny sill for two to four weeks or warm it very gently (a low double-boiler) for a few hours. Strain through cloth. **Dried material is safest** — water left in fresh herbs can spoil the oil (St John's Wort, made from fresh flowers, is the traditional exception, and should be watched). Calendula, yarrow, comfrey (topical), and St John's Wort all make classic infused oils.

### Salve — oil made solid
Warm one part beeswax into roughly four to five parts infused oil until melted, stir, and pour into tins or jars to set. Adjust the wax for a firmer or softer balm. A calendula salve is the natural first one to make — gentle, useful, and forgiving.

### Syrup — for berries, for winter
Make a strong decoction of the cooked berries, strain, then dissolve in honey or sugar (a common ratio is roughly equal parts liquid to honey) to preserve it. Elderberry syrup is the classic; rowan and crab apple make sharp, pectin-rich versions.

### Oxymel — honey and vinegar
Steep herb or berry in a mix of honey and cider vinegar for a few weeks, then strain. A pleasant, keeping way to take elderberry or herbs through the cold months.

### Poultice — the simplest remedy of all
Crush or bruise fresh leaf and apply directly to the skin, held with a cloth. The oldest use of yarrow (to a small cut) and comfrey (to a bruise or sprain, on unbroken skin) is exactly this.

## A gentle first project

If I want one place to begin: in summer, I dry a good picking of **calendula** flowers, infuse them into sweet almond oil on a sunny sill for a few weeks, strain, and warm the oil with a little beeswax into a **simple calendula salve**. It's soothing, broadly useful, hard to get wrong, and it turns a windowsill of flowers into something I made with my own hands — which is, after all, the whole point.
