# Pollinators & bees

*Welcoming the birds, bees, and butterflies to my sky garden — and an honest, careful look at whether a beehive belongs on my balcony.*

The plants in this garden were chosen partly for the life they draw. A balcony twenty feet by five can become a genuine stopping-place for pollinators moving through the city, and the small creatures that arrive are part of what makes it feel alive.

---

## Feeding the pollinators through the year

Pollinators need forage across the whole season, not just a burst in June. This collection is arranged to provide a **succession of bloom**:

- **Early (spring):** blackthorn's very early blossom and primrose's first flowers, then crab apple, hawthorn, and rowan blossom, and wild garlic's star-flowers.
- **Mid (summer):** elder, meadowsweet, foxglove, St John's Wort, yarrow, vervain, valerian, chamomile, lemon balm, and the long-flowering calendula and comfrey.
- **Late (late summer into autumn):** heather, water-mint, and calendula and yarrow carrying on — the valuable last nectar of the year.

A few simple additions make a balcony markedly more welcoming: a **shallow dish of water with pebbles or marbles** for bees and butterflies to land on and drink safely; a small **bee house** for solitary native bees (below); and a preference for **open, single flowers** over frilly doubles, which often hide their nectar.

Two plants in the collection are bee-magnets by inheritance. **Lemon balm** takes its name *Melissa* from the Greek for honeybee, and was rubbed on hives for centuries to keep bees content and draw a swarm home. And an old Irish charm, recorded by Lady Wilde, to gather bees to a hive called for mint, **chamomile**, and **valerian** among other herbs boiled with honey and rubbed inside and out on the empty vessel — "and the bees will soon come." A century of folklore and a windowsill of the right flowers point the same way.

## For the butterflies

Nectar feeds adult butterflies, but caterpillars need host plants. The **nettle** in this collection is a host for the small tortoiseshell and peacock — reason enough to keep a contained pot of it. Letting a corner grow a little wild, and avoiding pesticides entirely, does more for butterflies than anything bought.

## For the birds

The berrying trees — rowan, hawthorn, crab apple, elder — carry the birds through autumn and into winter, which is when food is scarcest and my fruit is most valued. A little water and a quiet, unhurried presence do the rest.

## On keeping bees: native bees first

There are two quite different things people mean by "keeping bees," and for a balcony the distinction matters.

**Solitary native bees** — such as mason bees — are the gentle, well-suited option. A small bee house mounted on a sheltered wall invites them; they rarely sting, need no management or registration, and are excellent pollinators. For a sky garden, this is very likely the right and rewarding choice, and a natural place to begin.

**A honeybee hive** is a different undertaking — closer to keeping livestock than hanging a bee house — and on a balcony it faces real, specific constraints. Worth reading the section below before I set my heart on it.

---

## Honeybees on a Vancouver balcony: what to know

Keeping honeybees in Vancouver is permitted, but it is bound by both provincial law and city guidelines, and several of those guidelines sit awkwardly with a balcony. This is worth understanding clearly and early, so that any decision is made with the neighbours' peace and the bees' welfare both in view.

**Provincial registration is mandatory.** Anyone keeping honeybees in British Columbia must register (free) with the BC Ministry of Agriculture, and register the location of the apiary. This is a legal requirement, in place to help control bee diseases and pests.

**The city's siting guidelines are the sticking point for a balcony.** Vancouver's guidance restricts hives to the **rear of a property**, away from street-facing sides, and calls for a secure enclosure or a **fence or hedge of at least six feet (1.8 m)** around the hive. The purpose of that height is to lift the bees' flight path up above head height quickly, so they clear people and neighbours — which is exactly the concern on a shared balcony. A front- or side-facing balcony, or one without room for that screening, may not meet the guidance at all.

**The city actively encourages preparation first.** Vancouver discourages taking up honey beekeeping before completing a recognised beekeeping course and joining a local bee club, and notes candidly that honeybees are a non-native, managed species that can compete with native pollinators for limited urban forage. The Provincial Ministry offers free introductory courses.

**Being a good neighbour is the whole game.** Even where it is permitted, a hive close to neighbours calls for careful placement of the entrance and flight path, a reliable water source so bees don't visit neighbours' taps, and swarm prevention through good management.

### An honest recommendation

For a balcony this size, shared with neighbours, **begin with a native-bee house and a garden full of forage.** It delivers much of the joy and the pollination with none of the siting difficulty, the registration, or the risk to a neighbour's goodwill. If a honeybee hive still calls to me afterwards, I'll treat it as a considered next step: take a recognised course, join a local club, confirm my specific balcony can meet the rear-facing and six-foot-screening guidance, register the apiary with the province, and speak with my building and my neighbours first. Approached that way, it can be done well — and only that way.

*City and provincial rules change; confirm the current requirements directly with the City of Vancouver and the BC Ministry of Agriculture before I act.*
